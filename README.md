# Quantum vs. Classical Portfolio Optimization

A side-by-side study of two different portfolio optimization problems on the same 11-asset ETF universe. We implement:

1. **Classical continuous Markowitz mean-variance optimization**, solved exactly via convex quadratic programming.
2. **Discrete "select B of N assets" combinatorial optimization**, formulated as a QUBO, mapped to an Ising Hamiltonian, and solved with the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) on a Qiskit simulator, benchmarked against exact brute-force enumeration and classical simulated annealing.

We find that QAOA is not a "speedup" of Markowitz. It solves a different problem: discrete subset selection rather than continuous capital allocation, for which it is a more appropriate candidate algorithm. This repo reports how well it does at that and its flaws.

## Assets selected:

11 sector/asset-class ETFs: `XLF` (Financials), `XLE` (Energy), `XLK` (Technology), `XLV` (Healthcare), `XLI` (Industrials), `XLY` (Consumer Discretionary), `XLP` (Consumer Staples), `XLU` (Utilities), `GLD` (Gold), `TLT` (Long-Term Treasuries), `VNQ` (REITs).

## TL;DR

Across every budget tested (B = 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 assets selected out of 11), **QAOA's best-of-5-multistart run found the exact global optimum**, matching both brute-force exact enumeration and classical simulated annealing. However, the probability of *sampling* that optimum on any single shot was low (typically well under 1%), and the fraction of all shots landing on any feasible (correct-cardinality) solution ranged from ~23% to ~62% across budgets with no clean trend. QAOA located the right answer; it did not concentrate confidently on it.

## Methodology

### 1. Data (`data_loader.py`)

Pulls 3 years of daily adjusted-close prices for all 11 ETFs via `yfinance`, computes daily log returns, then annualizes to get:
- **μ (mu)**: an 11-vector of annualized expected returns
- **Σ (Sigma)**: an 11×11 annualized covariance matrix

A rolling-window stability diagnostic is included to flag how much Σ shifts across sub-periods; a 3-year window is a reasonable middle ground between enough samples to estimate 55 free covariance parameters and not diluting the estimate with stale, regime-shifted data.

### 2. Classical baseline (`markowitz_baseline.py`)

Solves the standard Markowitz problem with **continuous** long-only weights:

- **Minimum-variance portfolio**: minimize `wᵀΣw` subject to `Σw = 1`, `w ≥ 0`
- **Maximum-Sharpe portfolio**: via the standard convex reformulation (Cornuejols & Tutuncu)
- **Efficient frontier**: minimum variance swept across a range of target returns

This answers "what fraction of capital to put in each asset" — a fundamentally different question from the discrete side of this project.

### 3. QUBO formulation (`qubo_formulation.py`)

The discrete problem: choose exactly B of the 11 assets (binary `wᵢ ∈ {0,1}`) to minimize risk while rewarding return. Formulated as:

```
H(w) = wᵀΣw − q·μᵀw + λ(Σwᵢ − B)²
```

- `wᵀΣw` — risk term among selected assets
- `−q·μᵀw` — return reward, `q` = risk-aversion trade-off
- `λ(Σwᵢ − B)²` — soft penalty enforcing the cardinality constraint (exactly B assets), since QUBO requires unconstrained binary optimization; the constraint has to be folded in as a penalty rather than enforced directly

**λ is chosen heuristically** off the scale of Σ and μ, then validated empirically: for every budget tested, the unconstrained QUBO argmin landed on a feasible (exactly-B) solution, confirming λ was neither too weak (which would let infeasible solutions win) nor so strong it swamped the risk/return landscape. This penalty-tuning sensitivity is a known soft spot of QUBO formulations generally, and is checked rather than assumed here.

The QUBO is then converted to an **Ising Hamiltonian** (qubit operator) via Qiskit's built-in converter — this is the object QAOA actually operates on. With 11 assets, this is an 11-qubit problem with full pairwise (66-term) connectivity, since the covariance matrix couples every pair of assets.

### 4. Classical reference solvers (`classical_solvers.py`)

- **Brute-force exact enumeration**: with N=11, there are only 2¹¹ = 2048 possible bitstrings, small enough to check every single one. This gives the **true global optimum** for every budget, which is the baseline everything else is measured against.
- **Classical simulated annealing**: a standard metaheuristic (multi-restart, geometric cooling, bit-flip neighborhood). At N=11 this is overkill since brute force already solves it exactly, but it establishes the benchmarking methodology that matters at larger N (30, 50, 100+ assets) where brute force stops being tractable and SA becomes the realistic classical competitor.

### 5. QAOA (`qaoa_solver.py`)

A **from-scratch QAOA implementation** built directly on Qiskit's modern V2 primitives (`EstimatorV2`/`SamplerV2`) and `QAOAAnsatz`, rather than the legacy `qiskit_algorithms.QAOA` wrapper class

The loop:
1. Build a parameterized `QAOAAnsatz` circuit (p=2 layers/"reps") for the Ising Hamiltonian.
2. A classical outer-loop optimizer (`scipy.optimize.minimize`, COBYLA) tunes the circuit's variational angles (β, γ) by repeatedly estimating ⟨H⟩ via `EstimatorV2` on the Aer simulator.
3. Once converged, sample the final circuit many times (`SamplerV2`, 4096 shots) to get a distribution over bitstrings.
4. Post-process: rank sampled bitstrings both by frequency and by actual QUBO energy, separately tracking whether each is *feasible* (exactly B ones).

QAOA is fundamentally a **sampling algorithm** — there's no guarantee the most-frequently-sampled bitstring is the lowest-energy one observed, and this repo reports both rather than conflating them.

### 6. Full benchmark (`run_full_benchmark.py`)

For each budget B ∈ {3, 4, 5, 6, 7}, runs brute force, simulated annealing, and **multi-start QAOA** (5 random initializations, best-converged run kept) and reports:
- Whether each method's solution matches the true optimum
- QAOA's energy gap to optimal
- The fraction of QAOA's sampled shots that were feasible
- Circuit depth/size
- Runtime

Multi-start matters: a single QAOA run can converge to a mediocre local optimum of the variational landscape; running several random initializations and keeping the best is standard practice and is what's reported here as "QAOA's" result, not a cherry-picked single seed.

*Takeaways:*

- QAOA (p=2, COBYLA, 5 random restarts) reliably **located** the true combinatorial optimum at every budget tested on this 11-asset, fully-connected problem; however, it didn't confidentally concetrate probability on the optimum
- Feasible-shot fraction (fraction of all 4096 shots landing on *any* valid B-asset selection) varied from 22.7% to 61.5% with no clear pattern tied to B.
- A small side-experiment (not in the main driver script) found that increasing circuit depth (p=1 → 2 → 3) did **not** monotonically improve feasible-shot concentration at a fixed classical-optimizer iteration budget.

Sidenote: This is **simulator-only**. No real NISQ hardware noise is reflected in these numbers, as that's a separate, but known limitation

## Some more limitations

- **QUBO penalty tuning** is heuristic, empirically validated per-budget but not theoretically guaranteed to generalize to other λ scalings, risk-aversion parameters, or asset universes.
- **Equal-weight assumption**: once a subset of B assets is selected, this analysis evaluates it at equal weights — it does not re-optimize continuous weights within the selected subset, which would be a natural (and more realistic) extension.
- **No NISQ hardware noise** is modeled. Simulator results are a noiseless upper bound on what real quantum hardware would achieve.
- **N=11 is small.** Brute force is only tractable because there are just 2048 bitstrings to check; the entire benchmarking methodology (and the honest comparison to simulated annealing) is set up explicitly so it generalizes to larger N where brute force becomes infeasible.
- **3-year covariance window** showed real (if moderate) rolling instability in diagnostic checks — a longer or regime-aware estimation approach is a natural follow-up.
